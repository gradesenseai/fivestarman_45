Fivestarman 45-Day Challenge

Week Two

Discussion Guide · Days 8 through 14

A word before we start

Week 2 is where the work starts hitting the closer rings. Your marriage. Your prayer life. Your reputation. What you let your eyes settle on. The first week was about getting in motion. This one tests whether the motion holds when it gets personal.

Each day below carries a verse, the discussion question, and a handful of example angles to bring to the table if the room goes quiet. No prepared answers this week. Bring your own.

Day 08 · Tuesday May 13

Be a Blessing to Your Wife

Gallant
Summary

Your wife is a gift from God, not a burden, and not a replacement for His presence in your life. Joseph took Mary as his wife in obedience and trust, not in fear. A Gallant man honors that his wife is both his bride and a spiritual sister with her own walk with the Father. He blesses his marriage by keeping passion alive, celebrating her wins, living to impress her, and honoring her relationship with God.

Verse

An angel of the Lord appeared to him in a dream and said, "Joseph son of David, do not be afraid to take Mary home as your wife..."

Matthew 1:20
Discussion Question

In what practical ways can you shift your mindset to consistently view your wife as a gift rather than a source of stress or obligation?

  • Catching yourself in a frustration moment with her and pausing long enough to remember she's a gift before you respond
  • Naming out loud to her what you're thankful for about her, especially the things you usually just notice silently
  • Praying for her relationship with God instead of just praying for your marriage
  • Taking something off her plate without being asked or thanked for it
  • Choosing to be impressed by her in a season where it's easier to be impressed by your own work
  • Identifying the fear that's quietly running in your marriage (failure, disappointing her, being seen) and bringing it into the light instead of hiding it
Day 09 · Wednesday May 14

The Authentic Man Prays

Faithful
Summary

Prayer is the foundation of relationship with God, not a religious exercise. Jesus gave us a framework in Matthew 6. Six movements that train a man to pray with structure and honesty: Protocol & Paternal, Priority & Peace, Provision, Pardon, Protection & Power, Praise. The Faithful man learns to walk and pray this outline out loud, not as a script but as a launching pad. The lopsided parts of our prayer life, what we over-pray and what we skip, reveal where we trust God and where we're still trying to hold the line ourselves.

Verse

Then Jesus told his disciples a parable to show them that they should always pray and not give up.

Luke 18:1
Discussion Question

Which element of the Lord's Prayer framework (Protocol, Priority, Provision, Pardon, Protection, Praise) do you find most difficult to pray through honestly, and why?

  • Priority & Peace. When "Your will be done" actually means surrendering the outcome you're gripping
  • Provision. Asking honestly instead of pretending you don't have a need, or pretending the need is bigger than it is
  • Pardon. Being specific about what you actually did, and naming who you still haven't forgiven
  • Protection & Power. Admitting where the enemy actually has a foothold instead of praying vague "protect us" prayers
  • Praise. Praising God in the hard moment, not waiting until after the breakthrough
  • Which one you over-pray probably reveals what feels most like your job. Which one you skip reveals what you don't fully trust Him with yet.
Day 10 · Thursday May 15

Help a Stranger

Philanthropic
Summary

The Good Samaritan didn't run a background check on the man in the ditch. He saw the need, crossed the road, bandaged the wounds, and paid the cost. An authentic man has compassion on those who are hurting, and we're never more like Christ than when we help a stranger in trouble. The Philanthropic passion calls a man to stop asking "are they worthy?" and start asking "am I available?" Strangers are neighbors. Proximity and margin are the levers. Without them, the compassion never gets a chance to act.

Verse

He went to him and bandaged his wounds, pouring on oil and wine. Then he put the man on his own donkey, brought him to an inn and took care of him.

Luke 10:34
Discussion Question

What internal barriers (judgment, fear, inconvenience) most often stop you from acting on the impulse to help someone in need?

  • The judgment thought ("are they going to spend this on drugs?") and how it shifts the moral weight from you to them
  • The "I'm in a hurry" reflex that turns strangers into background instead of neighbors
  • The unspoken fear of being taken advantage of or looking foolish
  • The assumption that someone else will handle it. The bystander effect dressed up as practicality.
  • Not carrying cash on you, which is just a logistical excuse for not being prepared to give
  • Staying inside your house, your car, your routine. Proximity gaps that quietly disqualify you from being used.
Day 11 · Friday May 16

Get In The Game

Adventurous
Summary

Jonathan faced impossible odds with nothing but an armor bearer and the conviction that "there's no rule that says God can only deliver by using a big army." He moved. The Adventurous Spirit refuses to wait for perfect conditions to compete. Sports (softball, basketball, golf, tennis, hunting, running, pickleball, whatever) train a man to face fear, work inside something bigger than himself, and discover that the heroic is closer at hand than he thinks. Get off the sideline. Pick a sport. Get back in the game.

Verse

Perhaps the Lord will act in our behalf. Nothing can hinder the Lord from saving, whether by many or by few.

1 Samuel 14:6
Discussion Question

How has competition or team participation shaped your character in ways that carried over into your personal or professional life?

  • Learning to lose without making excuses, and how that shows up in a sales loss, a rejection, or a hard quarter
  • Discovering you can do more than you thought when a teammate is counting on you
  • The discipline of practice when nobody's watching, and how it built work habits that compound
  • Getting comfortable with being coached, corrected, and called out without taking it personally
  • The moment a sport humbled you and what it taught you about pride, ego, or anger
  • The pressure-release valve a competitive environment gives you, and what happens when you stop having one
Day 12 · Saturday May 17

Art of War

Entrepreneurial
Summary

If the trumpet doesn't sound a clear call, no one knows what battle to prepare for. Business is economic warfare, and clarity is the prerequisite for any strategy worth executing. Kennedy pulls from the U.S. military's nine principles of war (Objective, Offensive, Mass, Economy of Force, Maneuver, Unity of Command, Security, Surprise, Simplicity) as a framework for how a man actually leads his work. If you can't plan the next 24 hours with clarity, you won't plan the next 24 years.

Verse

Again, if the trumpet does not sound a clear call, who will get ready for battle?

1 Corinthians 14:8
Discussion Question

Which of the nine Battle Studies principles is most lacking in how you currently approach your work or business, and what would it look like to strengthen it?

  • Objective. Naming the one specific outcome everything else aligns behind, instead of a vague mission
  • Mass. Concentrating firepower on the right 10 percent instead of spreading thin across every opportunity
  • Economy of Force. Knowing what to automate, delegate, or stop doing entirely
  • Unity of Command. Who actually makes the call when a decision has to happen fast
  • Security. Naming your single biggest vulnerability (cash flow, key client, one platform) and protecting it
  • Surprise. What's your edge that competitors can't see or copy quickly
  • Simplicity. Can a brother repeat your strategy back to you in three sentences
Day 13 · Sunday May 18

Steward Your Influence

Gallant
Summary

Influence is currency. A good reputation is worth more than the most expensive perfume, and when a man walks into a room, his presence is a fragrance shaped by how he's treated people up to that moment. A Gallant man stewards that influence on purpose. He dresses to communicate respect, speaks clearly, uses people's names, avoids the cheap laugh at someone else's expense, listens more than he talks, and doesn't grasp for attention. The "fragrance" you leave behind is the cumulative weight of a thousand small choices.

Verse

A good name is more desirable than great riches; to be esteemed is better than silver or gold.

Proverbs 22:1
Discussion Question

If the people in your closest circles described the "fragrance" you bring into a room, what do you think they would honestly say?

  • What your wife would say versus what your coworkers would say, and whether there's a gap between the two
  • The room where your fragrance is weakest (usually the one where you're most tired or least guarded)
  • Whether people lean in when you start talking, or politely wait for you to finish
  • Whether you leave people feeling seen, or just registered that you were there
  • The difference between being "the funny guy" and being a man whose words actually carry weight
  • Listening as the real test. Not staying quiet while others talk, but letting what they said change what you say next.
Day 14 · Monday May 19

Bounce Your Eyes

Faithful
Summary

The eyes are the gatekeepers of the thoughts. A man cannot flourish in his marriage while his affections are divided through his gaze. Modern culture is saturated with sexual imagery. Advertisers can't sell a hamburger without it. The cost of letting that imagery linger is real. It splits the heart and undermines purity. The "bounce" is a learned, repeatable skill. When an attractive woman walks into the room, when a commercial uses sex to sell, when a thumbnail tries to pull you in, you turn away. You don't gaze. Job made a covenant with his eyes. The decision is made in advance, not in the moment.

Verse

But I tell you that anyone who looks at a woman lustfully has already committed adultery with her in his heart.

Matthew 5:28
Discussion Question

What specific environments or habits in your daily life make guarding your eyes most difficult, and what is one concrete change you could make?

  • Late-night TV when you're tired, alone, and your defenses are at their lowest
  • The phone in the bedroom after a certain hour. Most slip-ups happen there.
  • Algorithm-driven feeds that keep serving sexualized content even when you didn't ask for it
  • Specific apps, sites, or streaming platforms that are repeat offenders
  • The gym, the pool, the office. Places where the bounce has to be practiced in real time.
  • Pre-deciding what you'll watch before you sit down, instead of channel-surfing or letting autoplay choose
  • Making a covenant like Job. A real decision in advance, not white-knuckling it in the moment.
  • An accountability partner, a filter on the home network, or removing the device from the room entirely
